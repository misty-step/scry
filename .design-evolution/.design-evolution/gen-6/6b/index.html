Explanation

Spaced repetition leverages two well-established cognitive phenomena: the spacing effect and active recall. The spacing effect demonstrates that information is better retained when study sessions are distributed over time rather than massed together. Active recall requires actively stimulating memory during learning—retrieving information strengthens neural pathways more effectively than passive review.

By combining these principles, spaced repetition systems like Scry optimize the timing of each review to occur just as you\'re about to forget, maximizing efficiency and long-term retention.

Why This Matters

Continuous exposure (massed practice) actually produces worse long-term retention compared to spaced repetition. While it may feel easier and create a sense of familiarity, the learning is more fragile. The "fluency illusion" makes you think you know the material, but without the effort of retrieval after a delay, memories don\'t consolidate properly.
